Instructional Strategies

Methods

I make use of a variety of different educational methods in order to maximize student engagement and encourage active learning. Here are some of the activities I have done with students:

  • Textbook exercises
  • Additional worksheets that I find or create
  • Creative writing assignments
  • PowerPoint Presentations
  • Whiteboard exercises, where I have all students write their answers on whiteboards
  • Acting out Latin sentences in groups (e.g., having one student be the subject and another being the direct object)
  • Review Jeopardy
  • Other Review Games (e.g., Kahoot, Gimkit, Blooket)
  • Edpuzzles (especially for history, culture, and mythology)
  • Online review exercises (where student receives immediate feedback)


Differentiation

While student teaching, some of my differentiation strategies have involved providing students with options. Here are some examples:
  • In Latin III, I gave students the option of completing a creative writing assignment that covered the vocab and grammar for the chapter as an alternative to doing some or all of their textbook exercises.
  • In Latin V, I provided students with a variety of options for unit projects for my Julius Caesar unit. See here.
  • In various courses, I have given students the option of writing their answers on whiteboards rather than saying them aloud.
In the future, I hope to think of more ways to provide students with more agency in their education. Another form of differentiation is simply making use of a wide variety of different types of activities, and some of the activities that I have made use of are described above under "Methods."

My time at Central Dauphin helped me to better understand another element of differentiation. At that school, the teacher made more of an effort to make her classes accessible to a wider range of students, and, as a result, there was more variation in terms of skill level (many more IEP students, including students in learning support). Through this experience, I learned to tailor questions to students' skill levels. For instance, if I am working on a translation, I might ask one student to simply try their best to translate a clause. With another student, I may walk through each of the steps one by one or ask the student specific questions about the words in the sentence before moving on to a translation. Another differentiation strategy is to come up with additional enrichment tasks for students who finish tasks earlier than others.
