Research Papers (Classics)

Introduction

During my time at Kent State University, I completed many research papers in the field of classics, most of which focus on Latin texts. First and foremost, I would like to present my lengthy Master's thesis, entitled "Narratives of Paradise, Decline, and Restoration In Roman and Early Christian Texts: A Comparison," which is available here.


Shorter Papers

  • Commentary on Aeneid 4.331-387 - A commentary on the conversation between Aeneas and Dido after Dido learns of his intent to leave Carthage. This is not a research paper per se, but it was my first major project at Kent. This was the first class I took at Kent, and I took it as an undergraduate class. It was while taking this class, that my professor recommended that I apply for Kent's MA program.
  • The Miles Gloriosus in Plautus and Terrence: A Farcical Display of Masculinity - This paper examines the stock character of the miles gloriosus ("boastful soldier") found in Latin comedy in light of Roman conceptions of masculinity.
  • Virtus, Humanitas, and Caesar's Characterization of Gauls and Germans - This paper looks at the way in which Julius Caesar characterizes the Gauls and the Germans with respect to the traditional Roman Virtus of virtus and humanitas
  • Vergil and Tibullus on the Golden Age: A Comparison of Ideals - This paper compares discussions of "The Golden Age," a concept dating back at least as far as the epic poet Hesiod, in the works of the epic poet Vergil and the elegaic poet Tibullus respectively. The work I did with on this paper helped to inspire my thesis topic.
  • Mark Antony versus the Republic: Cicero's Portrayal of His Conflict with Mark Antony in the Second Philippic - In this paper, I examine Cicero's famous harangue against Mark Antony in his Second Philippic.
  • Shifting Gender Characterizations of Aeneas and Dido in Vergil's Aeneid - This paper focuses on the final meeting between Aeneas and Dido in the underworld and the ways in which their roles in the conversation, which reflect traditional gender stereotypes, have been reversed.
  • Commodus-Hercules as Subduer of Chaos - This paper examines the ways in which the Roman emperor Commodus was depicted in Latin inscriptions and how these inscriptions relate to Commodus's self-presentation as the Roman Hercules.
